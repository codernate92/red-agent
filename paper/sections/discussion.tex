\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The most important substantive pattern in the archived run is the separation
between evaluator-grounded harmful compliance and aggregate campaign risk.
\texttt{claude-sonnet-4} topped the CVSS-like risk score, but the clearest
qualitative jailbreak traces and the highest \sr\ scores came from the two
reachable Mistral models. This is exactly why the paper reports multiple
metrics: a model can expose more structurally important techniques overall while
another produces more concrete unsafe completions on the attacks that do land.

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{frontier.pdf}
  \caption{Risk-versus-\sr\ frontier for the executed models. The figure makes
  the metric split visible: Claude models concentrate aggregate risk, while
  Mistral models occupy the higher-\sr\ region.}
  \label{fig:frontier}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:frontier} makes this split explicit. The Claude cluster sits higher on
aggregate risk, whereas the two Mistral models move toward the upper-right
region on \sr\ and hit rate, at the cost of much worse error behavior. From an
engineering perspective, this suggests that a real red-team report should not
collapse everything into a single scalar. The operator who needs to decide
whether to file a disclosure, harden a tool boundary, or debug a provider setup
needs to know whether they are looking at refusal weakness, concrete unsafe
content, or a brittle inference path.

The second important point is methodological. Research papers often abstract
away the conditions that prevented an evaluation from running. In live systems
work, those conditions are part of the measurement. The blocked Together,
OpenAI, and Google aliases tell us something operationally meaningful: a
multi-provider safety framework needs credential hygiene, quota awareness, and
registry maintenance just to produce the evaluation matrix one might naively
assume is available by default.

Finally, the new engine package points toward a more disciplined future workflow
than the archived comparison path alone. The code now supports recon-led attack
surface ranking, confirmation reruns, and disclosure routing. Once that engine
is exercised in a live sweep over a healthier provider set, the framework should
be able to move from broad campaign comparison toward more targeted,
reproducible vulnerability discovery.
