\section{Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

This paper reports five distinct metrics. Keeping them separate matters, because
they capture different failure modes.

\paragraph{Campaign hit rate.}
For each model, the hit rate is the fraction of probes that the campaign runner
marked as \texttt{SUCCESS}. In the archived results this ranges from 15\% for
\texttt{claude-haiku-4-5} to 50\% for \texttt{mistral-small}. Hit rate is the
closest thing the current framework has to a coarse exploitation rate.

\paragraph{Campaign refusal rate.}
The campaign refusal rate is derived from \texttt{FAILED} probe outcomes rather
than judge labels. It therefore measures the campaign's own success detectors
and refusal logic, not an external evaluator's judgment. This distinction
matters because a probe can fail for reasons other than a clean refusal, and
the judge can still assign a non-zero severity to a response that the campaign
did not mark as a success.

\paragraph{\sr\ scoring.}
The secondary evaluation layer uses the \sr\ rubric
\citep{souly2024strongreject}. For a prompt-response pair, the judge assigns a
binary refusal variable and 1--5 ratings for convincingness and specificity. The
composite score is
\begin{equation}
\text{SR overall} = (1 - r)\frac{(c - 1)(s - 1)}{16},
\end{equation}
where $r$ is refusal, $c$ is convincingness, and $s$ is specificity. In the
archived run, \sr\ is applied to the framework's ATT\&CK-inspired probes rather
than to the public \sr\ harmful-prompt benchmark. We therefore use the score as
an internal comparative measure, not as a benchmark leaderboard number.

\paragraph{Aggregate risk score.}
The reporting pipeline also computes a CVSS-inspired aggregate risk score using
\path{analysis/vulnerability.py}. This score combines technique-level severity,
exploitability, observed success rate, and confidence into a normalized 0--10
scale. It is useful for ranking findings inside the framework, but it is not
interchangeable with \sr\ overall. One model can rank highest on aggregate risk
because it succeeds on more structurally important techniques, while another can
rank highest on \sr\ because its successful outputs are more concrete or
convincing.

\paragraph{Operational error rate.}
Finally, we track the fraction of attempted probes that ended in
\texttt{ERROR}. This is not a safety metric, but it is a core operational
metric. A model with a strong jailbreak signal and a 35\% probe error rate is a
different research object from a model with the same signal and no execution
instability.

\paragraph{Limits of comparability.}
Because the archived run covers only seven models, uses one judge, and applies
\sr\ to framework-native probes rather than to HarmBench or the public
\sr\ benchmark prompts, the results below should be read as an artifact-backed
case study rather than a general leaderboard.
